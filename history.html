History of Negele Borana

The town of Negele Borana was founded in the early 20th century. In 1934, it was described as an "Amhara new settlement and fortress with palisades," serving as the "farthest outpost towards Jubbaland."

During the Second Italo-Ethiopian War in 1935, Negele Borana was the headquarters of Ras Desta Damtew and faced frequent Italian bombing raids. General Rodolfo Graziani's forces captured the town after their victory at the Battle of Ganale Dorya.

British forces occupied the town on March 27, 1941, finding it abandoned and looted by the neighboring Borena Oromo. By 1943, David Buxton noted an Ethiopian Army battalion garrisoned in the "half-built Italian settlement."

The Norwegian Lutheran Mission established a hospital in Negele Borana in 1949, operating it until 1956 when the Ministry of Public Health took over. In 1958, Negele Borana was recognized as a First Class Township.

On January 12, 1974, soldiers stationed at the Military Base protested substandard living conditions, leading to the brief detention of Lieutenant-General Deresse Dubale. This incident, hushed up at the time, highlighted the severe conditions in remote garrisons.

During the Ogaden War in August 1977, the Somali Army repeatedly attempted to capture Negele Borana, but the local garrison successfully defended the town.

More recently, around January 2010, human rights activists reported numerous detentions in Negele Borana, primarily of individuals associated with the Oromo People's Congress. These arrests were reportedly linked to protests against mining activities in the region, which authorities attributed to opposition groups.
